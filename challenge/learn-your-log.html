1 Foundation

Why Maintenance Logs Are Not Optional

Every time a technician fixes a machine without writing it down, they make life harder for everyone who comes after them — including themselves, six months later when the same fault returns.

In a regulated industrial environment, maintenance records are a legal requirement. Under PSSR 2000 (Pressure Systems Safety Regulations), LOLER, and the Machinery Directive, operators must demonstrate that equipment was maintained to the manufacturer's schedule and any faults were correctly rectified. Without records, you have no defence in an HSE inspection or an insurance claim.

Real-World Consequence
In 2019, a UK food manufacturer was fined £1.2 million after a conveyor drive failure injured two workers. The prosecution succeeded in part because maintenance records were incomplete — the company could not prove the drive had been inspected as required. A 15-minute log entry would have changed the outcome.

Beyond compliance, good logs are how maintenance teams get smarter over time. Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF) calculations — used to predict when components will fail and plan proactive replacements — are only as accurate as the logs that feed them. Teams that log well can typically reduce unplanned downtime by 20–35% within two years.

Finally, logs protect you personally. If a machine you worked on fails catastrophically and hurts someone, your signed log entry showing exactly what you did and verified is the difference between "doing your job" and "negligence".

2 Anatomy

The 8 Fields Every Maintenance Log Must Contain

A maintenance log is not a diary — it is a structured technical record. The following eight fields are the minimum required by most industrial quality management systems (ISO 9001, IEC 62061 Annex C) and internal audit standards.

FIELD 01
Date & Time (Start and End)
Not just the date — the time the job started AND finished. Downtime duration is used to calculate the cost of the failure and justify capital replacements.
FIELD 02
Work Order Number
Links the log to the CMMS (Computerised Maintenance Management System) scheduling entry. Without this, the record is an orphan — invisible to planners and auditors.
FIELD 03
Equipment Tag / Asset ID
"The pump in Bay 3" is not an Asset ID. "P-101-B3" is. Every piece of plant has a unique tag. Use it — so the record attaches to the correct asset history.
FIELD 04
Fault Description
What failed, how was it reported (operator call, SCADA alarm, scheduled inspection), and what observable symptoms were present. Error codes included verbatim.
FIELD 05
Root Cause Analysis
The ACTUAL cause — not the symptom. "Motor tripped" is a symptom. "Bearing race worn due to lubrication interval missed at last scheduled PM" is a root cause.
FIELD 06
Corrective Action Taken
Step-by-step description of exactly what was done. Assume someone who was not there needs to verify your work or undo it. Be specific enough that they can.
FIELD 07
Parts Replaced (with Part Numbers)
Part number, description, quantity, and serial/batch number where traceable. This feeds procurement lead-time data and warranty claims. "New bearing" is not sufficient.
FIELD 08
Test & Verification Results
What was done to confirm the fix worked? Functional test, current measurement, insulation resistance, pressure test — with pass/fail criteria and actual readings recorded.
