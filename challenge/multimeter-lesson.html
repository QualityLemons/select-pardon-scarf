SECTION 01

WHAT IS A MULTIMETER?

A digital multimeter (DMM) is a handheld test instrument that measures electrical quantities — voltage, resistance, and current — using a single device. In PLC environments, it is the primary tool for verifying wiring, diagnosing field device faults, and confirming power supply health.

SECTION 02

THE DIAL — MEASUREMENT MODES

The rotary dial is the most important control on the meter. Select the wrong mode and you can damage the meter, blow its internal fuse, or — on live mains — put yourself in danger.

V⎓
DC VOLTAGE (VDC)
Measures steady-state direct current voltage. Used for: 24V PLC supply rails, PLC I/O card terminals, sensor signal voltages, battery checks.
Typical PLC range: 20V or 200V setting
V~
AC VOLTAGE (VAC)
Measures alternating current voltage (sinusoidal). Used for: mains input to PSU (230V/120V AC), transformer secondary outputs. ⚠ Highest risk mode.
Use 200V or 750V range for mains
Ω
RESISTANCE (OHM)
Measures resistance in ohms (Ω). Used for: solenoid coil checks (~100–500Ω), wire resistance (near 0Ω), contact resistance, motor winding checks. ALWAYS de-energise before measuring resistance.
PLC ranges: 200Ω, 2kΩ, 200kΩ
)))
CONTINUITY
Beeps audibly if resistance < ~50Ω between the two probes. Used for: confirming wire runs, checking fuse integrity, verifying switch contacts. Fastest diagnostic — eyes-free use.
Beep = connected, Silence/OL = open
⊢|◁
DIODE TEST
Measures the forward voltage drop of a diode or LED (typically 0.5–0.7V for silicon). Used for: checking freewheeling diodes across relay coils, LED indicators.
Good silicon diode: 0.5–0.7V
mA⎓
DC CURRENT (ADC)
Measures current flow. Must be connected in SERIES — the meter becomes part of the circuit. In-line connection only. Use 10A socket for high currents or you will blow the internal fuse.
Use 10A socket for currents > 200mA
SECTION 03

MEASURING DC VOLTAGE

DC voltage measurement is the most common task in a PLC panel. You use it to confirm a 24V supply rail is healthy, verify that an output is driving its load, or check that a sensor is signalling.

1
INSERT PROBES INTO CORRECT SOCKETS
Black probe → COM socket. Red probe → VΩmA socket. This never changes for voltage measurement.
2
SET DIAL TO V⎓ (DC VOLTAGE)
Select the range that is above your expected voltage. For a 24V PLC rail, select the 200V DC range. If the reading is small, switch to 20V for more precision.
💡 If you don't know the voltage, always start on the highest range to protect the meter.
3
PLACE BLACK PROBE FIRST
Touch the black (−) probe to the 0V common / GND rail first. This provides your reference point. Keep it stable — don't let it slip.
⚠ On live panels, maintain a firm grip. Use test clips if hands-free measurement is needed.
4
TOUCH RED PROBE TO TEST POINT
Touch the red (+) probe to the terminal you want to measure — e.g., the +24V supply output, a PLC input terminal, or an output coil terminal.
5
READ THE DISPLAY
The meter shows the DC voltage. A negative reading means your probes are reversed — swap them or note that current flows the other way.
6
INTERPRET THE RESULT
For a 24V DC system, an acceptable reading is typically 22.5V to 25.5V (±6%). A reading below 20V under load indicates a weak PSU or excessive voltage drop.

SECTION 04

RESISTANCE & CONTINUITY

Resistance measurement and continuity testing let you verify wiring integrity and component health without powering the circuit. They are your go-to tools for diagnosing broken wires, blown fuses, and failed solenoid coils.

1
⚠ DE-ENERGISE THE CIRCUIT FIRST
Resistance measurement injects a small test current from the meter's battery. If the circuit is live, the reading will be meaningless and you may damage the meter. Lock out / tag out before probing.
⛔ Never measure resistance on a live circuit. OL display with known-good wiring often means the circuit is still powered.
2
SET DIAL — Ω MODE
Select the resistance range. Start with 200Ω for wires and contacts, 2kΩ for solenoid coils (~100–500Ω), 200kΩ for high-impedance checks.
💡 For a simple wire continuity check, use ))) CONT mode instead — the audible beep means you don't have to watch the display.
3
PLACE PROBES — EITHER WAY AROUND
Unlike voltage, resistance is non-polar. Place one probe at each end of the component or wire segment you want to check. Order doesn't matter.
4
READ AND INTERPRET
Wire / fuse: Should read <1Ω (near zero). Higher reading = corrosion, loose crimp, or break.
Solenoid coil: Typically 50–500Ω depending on voltage rating. OL = open/burnt coil.
E-Stop button (NC): Should read ~0Ω when released, OL when pressed.

SECTION 05

⚠ SAFETY RULES

A multimeter used correctly is safe. Used incorrectly near mains voltage, it is lethal. These rules are non-negotiable on any industrial site.

⚡ MANDATORY SAFETY RULES
01
Check the meter's CAT rating. Always use a CAT III or CAT IV rated meter in industrial panel environments. CAT I meters are for low-energy electronics only.
02
Inspect leads before every use. Cracked insulation, exposed copper, or burnt probe tips must be replaced immediately. A broken lead at 230V AC is fatal.
03
Set the dial before touching the probes to any circuit. Switching modes with probes live can cause arcing if switching from Ω/CONT to VAC accidentally.
04
When measuring AC voltage: insert black probe first, then red. Remove red first, then black. This minimises the risk of accidental contact between the probes.
05
Never exceed the meter's input ratings. Most DMMs are rated 1000V DC or 750V AC max, but internal fuses limit current measurement to 10A (fused). Exceeding this blows the fuse — or worse.
06
Use one hand only on live measurements. Keep the other hand in your pocket or behind your back. This prevents current from passing across your chest if you accidentally contact live terminals.
07
Wear appropriate PPE — insulated gloves rated for the voltage, safety glasses. On mains-voltage panels, a face shield is required under IEC 61482.
SECTION 06

PLC FAULT-FINDING GUIDE

Follow this structured approach with the multimeter when a PLC system isn't behaving as expected. Always work from power source to load — confirm each stage is healthy before moving further.

SYMPTOM WHERE TO PROBE MODE EXPECTED / ACTION
Output coil won't drive load PLC Q terminal → 0V common VDC 20 Should read ~24V when rung true. 0V = rung is false or output card failed.
Load coil doesn't energise even with 24V at PLC output Solenoid terminals A–B Ω 2kΩ Expect 50–500Ω. OL = open coil (burnt). 0Ω = short circuit.
PLC input never goes HIGH despite switch pressed Input terminal → 0V common VDC 20 Should read ~24V with switch closed. 0V = wiring fault or switch not passing voltage.
Field switch suspected faulty Switch terminals (de-energised) CONT Beep when pressed (NO) or released (NC). No beep at expected state = switch failed.
Intermittent faults — suspected bad wire or connector Both ends of each wire CONT Beep = good. Wiggle wires during test. If beep drops = intermittent break.
24V PSU output low (< 22V under load) PSU DC output+ → DC common VDC 200 Good: 22.5–25.5V. Low: overloaded supply or failing capacitors — replace PSU.
No power to panel at all PSU AC input L → N VAC 200 Should read 230V (or 120V). 0V = upstream CB tripped or fuse blown. ⚠ Live mains.
Blown fuse suspected Fuse terminal IN → OUT (de-energised) CONT Beep = fuse good. Silence (OL) = fuse blown. Confirm with Ω 200: good fuse < 1Ω.
THE GOLDEN RULE
When a PLC output is ON (rung logic true, output LED lit on card) but the load doesn't operate:
1. Measure voltage at PLC output terminal → 0V common. Should be ~24V. If 0V, output card is blown.
2. If 24V at terminal, measure voltage at load terminals. If 0V, wire is broken between terminal block and load.
3. If 24V at load terminals but load doesn't work, de-energise and measure coil resistance. Likely open coil.
